Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about blood donation

Plasma is the liquid portion of blood that makes up about 55% of your blood volume. It's a straw-colored fluid that carries blood cells, nutrients, hormones, and proteins throughout your body.

Why plasma is important:

  • Contains essential proteins like albumin, immunoglobulins, and clotting factors
  • Helps maintain blood pressure and blood volume
  • Transports nutrients and waste products
  • Used to treat patients with bleeding disorders, immune deficiencies, and burns
  • Can be processed into life-saving medications

Stem cells are special cells that can develop into many different types of blood cells. They are found in bone marrow and peripheral blood.

Types of stem cell collection:

  • Peripheral Blood Stem Cells (PBSC): Collected through apheresis, similar to platelet donation
  • Bone Marrow: Collected through a surgical procedure under anesthesia

Used to treat:

  • Leukemia and lymphoma
  • Sickle cell disease
  • Severe immune system disorders
  • Some solid tumors

Most healthy adults can donate blood. Here's who can typically donate:

  • People aged 17+ (16 with parental consent in some states)
  • Weight at least 110 pounds
  • In good health and feeling well
  • Have adequate iron levels

Special considerations:

  • First-time donors: Welcome! We'll guide you through the process
  • Regular donors: Can donate whole blood every 56 days
  • Type O donors: Universal donors, especially needed
  • AB donors: Universal plasma donors

For detailed eligibility requirements, please visit our Check Eligibility page.

Impact on others:

  • One donation can save up to 3 lives
  • Blood is needed every 2 seconds in the US
  • Only 3% of age-eligible people donate blood yearly
  • Blood cannot be manufactured - it can only come from volunteers
  • Helps cancer patients, accident victims, surgical patients, and newborns

Benefits for you:

  • Free mini-physical and health screening
  • Learn your blood type and vital signs
  • Sense of community service and helping others
  • Regular health monitoring through donation screenings
  • Potential health benefits from regular donation

Immediately after donation:

  • Rest in the observation area for 10-15 minutes
  • Enjoy refreshments to help replenish fluids and energy
  • Keep the bandage on for at least 4 hours
  • Avoid lifting heavy objects with the donation arm for 4-6 hours

For the next 24 hours:

  • Drink extra fluids (non-alcoholic)
  • Eat healthy, iron-rich foods
  • Avoid strenuous exercise or activities
  • Take any prescribed iron supplements if recommended

When to contact us:

  • Persistent bleeding at the needle site
  • Severe or persistent dizziness
  • Signs of infection at the needle site
  • Any concerning symptoms

Blood types are determined by proteins on red blood cells. The main blood group systems are ABO and Rh.

Universal Donors:

  • O-: Universal red cell donor (can give to all types)
  • AB+: Universal plasma donor

Universal Recipients:

  • AB+: Can receive red cells from all types
  • O-: Can receive plasma from all types

Blood Type Distribution:

  • O+ (37.4%) - Most common
  • A+ (35.7%)
  • B+ (8.5%)
  • AB+ (3.4%)
  • O- (6.6%) - Universal donor
  • A- (6.3%)
  • B- (1.5%)
  • AB- (0.6%) - Rarest

Donation Safety:

  • All equipment is sterile and used only once
  • Cannot get infected from donating blood
  • Trained medical professionals oversee the process
  • Strict safety protocols are followed

Blood Testing:

Every donation is tested for:

  • ABO group and Rh type
  • Unexpected antibodies
  • Hepatitis B and C
  • HIV-1 and HIV-2
  • HTLV-I and HTLV-II
  • Syphilis
  • West Nile Virus
  • Chagas disease
  • Zika virus (when indicated)

Donation frequency depends on the type of donation:

  • Whole Blood: Every 56 days (8 weeks) - up to 6 times per year
  • Power Red (Double Red Cell): Every 112 days (16 weeks) - up to 3 times per year
  • Platelets: Every 7 days, up to 24 times per year
  • Plasma: Every 28 days (4 weeks) - up to 13 times per year

Why the waiting periods?

  • Allows your body to fully replenish donated components
  • Maintains your health and well-being
  • Ensures the quality of future donations
  • Prevents iron deficiency in regular donors

Processing Steps:

  1. Collection: Blood is collected in sterile bags with preservatives
  2. Testing: Samples are tested for blood type and infectious diseases
  3. Separation: Whole blood is separated into components:
    • Red blood cells
    • Plasma
    • Platelets
    • Cryoprecipitate (clotting factors)
  4. Storage: Components are stored at specific temperatures
  5. Distribution: Sent to hospitals and medical facilities
  6. Transfusion: Given to patients who need them

Shelf Life:

  • Red blood cells: 42 days
  • Platelets: 5 days
  • Plasma: 1 year when frozen
  • Cryoprecipitate: 1 year when frozen
